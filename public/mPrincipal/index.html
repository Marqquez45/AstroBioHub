The Problem We Solve

We developed a platform designed to present information generated by NASA that is currently unstructured and difficult to explore dynamically. Our solution addresses this problem by organizing content on specific topics with clear and accessible language, allowing users to request clarifications on parts they do not understand. Additionally, it enables researchers to conduct studies more efficiently and systematically.

This way, years of research are optimally summarized, facilitating faster decision-making and more effective research. This project is important because it facilitates access to research for casual users and improves the productivity of scientists, thereby enhancing understanding and discovery in the field of space biology.

What is AstroBioHub?

AstroBioHub transforms NASA's vast repository of space biology publications into accessible, understandable, and practical knowledge. By using artificial intelligence, natural language processing, semantic search, and knowledge graphs, it condenses hundreds of research articles into clear summaries, identifies key findings, highlights knowledge gaps, and provides information that helps both casual users and researchers explore space biology efficiently and effectively.

The Heart of AstroBioHub

AstroBioHub is a platform that helps scientists and researchers discover, analyze, and synthesize scientific literature intelligently. Instead of searching for articles one by one, researchers ask questions in natural language and receive deep analyses, connections between studies, and ready-to-use knowledge synthesis.

How Does It Work? - System's Logical Flow

Step 1: The Researcher Asks a Question

A scientist has a research question, for example:

"Which extremophilic organisms can survive in Mars-like conditions?"
"How does UV radiation affect bacteria in extreme environments?"

The researcher simply types their question into the platform. The system validates that the question is well-formed and accepts optional parameters such as date ranges or the desired number of results.

Step 2: The System Decides the Search Strategy

Not all questions require the same type of analysis. The system has an intelligent processing center that determines which strategy to use:

Four Available Strategies:
  • Keyword Search: For simple and direct searches. Finds exact term matches.
  • Hybrid Search: Combines keyword matches with intelligent ranking. Balances precision and coverage.
  • Semantic Search: Understands the meaning, not just the exact words. Finds related concepts even if they are expressed differently.
  • Deep Analysis with AI: For complex questions that require synthesis. Generates summaries, extracts patterns, and finds knowledge gaps.

Step 3: Intelligent Document Search

This is where the technical magic happens. The system uses two main techniques:

A) Semantic Expansion - Understanding Concepts, Not Just Words

The Problem: If I search for "extremophilic microorganisms" but a paper uses "thermophilic bacteria," a simple search would miss it.
The Solution: The system uses WordNet (a linguistic database) to expand concepts:
"extremophile" ‚Üí also includes: thermophile, halophile, acidophile, psychrophile
"microorganism" ‚Üí also includes: bacterium, archaea, microbe

Now the search finds all relevant documents, even if they use different terminology.

B) Intelligent Ranking with BM25 - Sorting by Real Relevance

The Problem: Not all articles that mention a term are equally relevant. We need a mathematical system that understands:
A term that appears many times in a short paper is more relevant than one that appears a few times in a long paper. Rare and specific terms are more informative than common terms.

The Solution - BM25 Algorithm: Imagine you have 1000 papers and are searching for "methanogens in ice":

  • Intelligent Term Frequency: If "methanogens" appears 5 times in a 100-word paper ‚Üí very relevant. If it appears 5 times in a 10,000-word paper ‚Üí less relevant. But there's a limit: it doesn't matter if it appears 50 or 100 times, we already understand it's central.
  • Term Rarity: "methanogens" appears in only 10 out of 1000 papers ‚Üí very specific term, high value. "in" appears in all papers ‚Üí provides no information, low value.
  • Length Normalization: Long papers naturally have more words, which doesn't mean they are more relevant. The system adjusts for this automatically.

Conceptual Formula (without complex math):
Relevance = How specific is this term? √ó How present is it in this specific document? √∑ How long is the document?

Advantages of this Approach: Fast, Accurate, and Cost-effective.

Step 4: Deep Analysis with Artificial Intelligence

For complex questions, the system sends the most relevant articles to an AI model (Google Gemini) that acts as an expert research assistant.

What Does the AI Do?

1. Structured Information Extraction: It reads each article and extracts: Organisms studied, Experimental conditions, Methods used, and Quantitative results.
Example: From the text: "Strains of Deinococcus radiodurans survived 15 kGy of gamma radiation at -80¬∞C" the AI extracts: Organism: Deinococcus radiodurans, Condition 1: Gamma radiation, 15 kGy, Condition 2: Temperature, -80¬∞C, Result: Survival confirmed.

2. Multi-Level Synthesis: The AI generates four types of summaries for different needs: Executive, Technical, Educational, and Structured.

3. Insight Discovery: The AI identifies Key Findings, Emerging Patterns, Contradictions, and Knowledge Gaps.
Example of Insight: "Three independent studies show that methanogens can survive at -196¬∞C, but none have tested their metabolic activity at those temperatures. This represents a critical research opportunity."

Step 5: Knowledge Graph Construction

The system doesn't just store information; it intelligently connects it like a network of concepts.

What is a Knowledge Graph?

Imagine a giant mind map where the Points (Nodes) are Articles, Authors, Organisms, etc., and the Lines (Connections) are the relationships between them.

Analytical Power of the Graph:

It allows for the discovery of research communities, identification of the most influential works, finding bridges between fields, and conceptual navigation (e.g., "Show all organisms studied under simulated Martian conditions").

Step 6: Visualization and Exploration

The results are presented interactively and visually through connection networks, timelines, collaboration maps, and structured summaries with automatic citations.

Step 7: Continuous Learning

The system improves with use through a feedback loop where it learns from the researcher's interactions to automatically adjust its algorithms and become more accurate.

Key System Advantages

  • Computational Efficiency: Does not require expensive GPUs to run. Fast searches even with thousands of documents.
  • Depth of Analysis: Combines speed (BM25) with intelligence (AI). Extracts information that would require days of manual reading.
  • Scalability: Designed to process thousands of papers and grow without architectural redesign.
  • Flexibility: Four search modes adapted to different needs and multiple output formats.

Impact for Research

Before AstroBioHub: Weeks reading papers, missed connections, difficult to synthesize knowledge.
With AstroBioHub: Intelligent synthesis in minutes, automatic discovery of relationships, semantic search that finds everything relevant.

Technical Foundations (Without Complexity)

  • Document Database (MongoDB): Stores the full text of articles.
  • Graph Database (Neo4j): Stores relationships between entities.
  • Natural Language Processing (NLTK + WordNet): Understands synonyms and relationships between words.
  • Language Model (Google Gemini): Advanced AI that "reads" papers and extracts information.
  • Ranking Algorithm (BM25): Proven mathematics for ordering documents by relevance.

Future of the System

Next Improvements: Optional Dense Embeddings, Reinforcement Learning, Multimodal Analysis, and Improved Explainability.

Conclusion

AstroBioHub transforms the way scientists interact with literature. It is not just a faster search engine - it is an intelligent research assistant that understands questions, finds information, analyzes content, synthesizes knowledge, connects ideas, and continuously learns to better serve researchers.

PHASE 2: VECTORIZATION (EMBEDDINGS)

Objective

Convert human-readable text into numerical vectors that computers can compare mathematically.

What is an Embedding?

Simple Analogy:

Imagine a map where each word is a point. Words with similar meanings are close together:

  • "bacteria" and "microorganism" ‚Üí close
  • "bacteria" and "rocket" ‚Üí distant

In reality:

Each text is converted into a vector of 768 numbers.

Text: "Bacteria survive in microgravity"

Embedding (simplified to 5 dimensions for visualization):
[0.234, -0.567, 0.891, 0.123, -0.445]

Real: 768 dimensions
[0.234, -0.567, 0.891, ..., -0.332] (768 numbers)

Why Vectors?

Because they allow for measuring similarity mathematically:

Vector A: "bacteria in space"      ‚Üí [0.8, 0.2, 0.1]
Vector B: "microbes in microgravity" ‚Üí [0.7, 0.3, 0.15]
Vector C: "rocket fuel composition"   ‚Üí [0.1, 0.9, 0.8]

Similarity (cosine):
  A vs B = 0.92 ‚Üí very similar ‚úÖ (same concept)
  A vs C = 0.15 ‚Üí not similar ‚úó (different concepts)

Model Used: S-PubMedBERT-MS-MARCO

Why this specific model?

It is an AI model trained on:

  1. PubMed (millions of biomedical papers) ‚Üí understands scientific terminology
  2. MS-MARCO (Microsoft search dataset) ‚Üí optimized for finding relevant information

Advantage over general models:

General model (like GPT):
  Query: "thermophilic archaea"
  May confuse with: "thermal architecture", "archaic themes"

S-PubMedBERT:
  Query: "thermophilic archaea"
  Understands: extremophilic organisms that live at high temperatures
  Finds: papers about Pyrococcus, Sulfolobus, etc.

How BERT Works (Simplified)

BERT uses a mechanism called "Self-Attention".

Central idea: To understand a word, you need the context of the words around it.

Example:

Sentence: "Bacteria survive extreme radiation"

What does "survive" mean?

BERT analyzes:
  - "bacteria" ‚Üí WHO survives? (subject)
  - "radiation" ‚Üí WHAT does it survive? (object)
  - "extreme" ‚Üí WHAT KIND of radiation? (modifier)

Calculate "attention weights":
  survive ‚Üí bacteria:  45% (high attention)
  survive ‚Üí radiation: 40% (high attention)
  survive ‚Üí extreme:   15% (low attention)

Final vector for "survive" = 
  45% info from "bacteria" + 40% info from "radiation" + 15% info from "extreme"

Mathematical Formula (core of BERT):

Attention ( Q , K , V ) = softmax ( Q √ó K T / ‚àöd ) √ó V

Where:

  • Q = Query: "What am I looking for?"
  • K = Key: "What information do I have?"
  • V = Value: "What information do I offer?"
  • d = 64 (dimensionality, for normalization)
  • softmax = function that converts numbers into probabilities (sum to 100%)

Numerical example:

The word "survive" seeks context from other words:

survive_query = [1.0, 0.5, 0.2]
bacteria_key =  [0.9, 0.6, 0.1]
radiation_key = [0.8, 0.4, 0.3]

Step 1: Calculate similarity (dot product)
  survive ¬∑ bacteria =  1.0√ó0.9 + 0.5√ó0.6 + 0.2√ó0.1 = 1.22
  survive ¬∑ radiation = 1.0√ó0.8 + 0.5√ó0.4 + 0.2√ó0.3 = 1.06

Step 2: Convert to probabilities (softmax)
  weight_bacteria =   e^1.22 / (e^1.22 + e^1.06) = 0.54 (54%)
  weight_radiation =  e^1.06 / (e^1.22 + e^1.06) = 0.46 (46%)

Result:
  The "survive" vector incorporates 54% of the context from "bacteria"
                             and 46% of the context from "radiation"

Index for Fast Searching: FAISS

Problem: With 4,856 vectors of 768 dimensions, how to search quickly?

Solution: FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search)

Specialized data structure:
  - Indexes vectors for search in <50ms
  - Uses approximations for large corpora (millions of documents)

Similarity Measurement: Cosine Similarity

Formula:

cosine_similarity ( A , B ) = ( A ¬∑ B ) / ( ||A|| √ó ||B|| )

Where:

  • A ¬∑ B = dot product = sum(A[i] √ó B[i]) for all dimensions
  • ||A|| = vector length = ‚àö(sum(A[i]¬≤))

Range: -1 to 1

  • 1.0 = identical vectors (maximum similarity)
  • 0.5 = similar vectors
  • 0.0 = perpendicular vectors (no relationship)
  • -1.0 = opposite vectors

Example:

Query: "bacteria in space" ‚Üí vector_q = [0.8, 0.3, 0.1]

Paper 1: "Bacterial growth in microgravity" ‚Üí vector_p1 = [0.7, 0.4, 0.15]
Paper 2: "Rocket propulsion systems" ‚Üí vector_p2 = [0.1, 0.2, 0.9]

Calculation:
  q ¬∑ p1 = 0.8√ó0.7 + 0.3√ó0.4 + 0.1√ó0.15 = 0.56 + 0.12 + 0.015 = 0.695
  ||q|| = ‚àö(0.8¬≤ + 0.3¬≤ + 0.1¬≤) = ‚àö(0.64 + 0.09 + 0.01) = ‚àö0.74 = 0.86
  ||p1|| = ‚àö(0.7¬≤ + 0.4¬≤ + 0.15¬≤) = ‚àö0.7125 = 0.84
  
  cosine(q, p1) = 0.695 / (0.86 √ó 0.84) = 0.695 / 0.72 = 0.96 ‚úÖ very relevant

  cosine(q, p2) = 0.13 ‚ùå not relevant

The system returns Paper 1 as the main result
